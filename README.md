## Reviewing your level and your final design questions (15 min)
Spend a little bit of time reviewing your level. This isn't a full playthough, but instead going over your level as a whole: your prototypes, what you've created in engine, your documentation, etc. What kind of level have you been trying to build? Is it a fast-paced, corridor-like game with lots of blinking? Is it something more open and ponderous? Often we can't quite see what we were going for until we're part way there, so think about this a little.

Then, consider: what are the remaining <i>design</i> questions you have? As per the lecture, these aren't the questions you'd ask the player but instead what the remaining unknowns about your level are. Formulate one (two at most) of these into formal questions. Look back over the lecture notes for this week for some boilerplates to get you started.

Your instructor will go around the room and ask you to brief the class on your design question(s). These will set the tone for what you are investigating in this session.

## Authoring instruments (20 min)
Before you bring some playtesters over, figure out your methods of data collection. You will want to consider both the method and the way you are going to collect this data. As you don't have a lot of time, we can be a bit "quick and dirty" with this, but should still follow good practice. Refer back to the lecture notes or discuss with your peers/instructor on what makes a good playtest.

Decide on your approach. Some ideas/considerations:

* Think Aloud Protocol - playtester is encouraged to narrate their thinking as they play, and the design records this.
* Semi-structured interview - a short interview after the player has completed the level, with some questions that lead to further discussion. The designer records this.
* Likert Scale - the playtester is asked questions with their answers being a ranking along a scale from 1-5/1-7. Can be done by hand or recorded using a tool like https://forms.office.com/
* Observation - the playtester's actions in game are noted by the designer, generally targetting particular behaviours.

Your method of recording data for your playtest may vary, and include writing down notes or audio recordings. Remember to seek consent from your playtester before recording them.

Generally, good playtesting requires use of multiple approaches at once and some sort of mix. However, as you are just learning, it is okay to pick one and roll with it for now. You might want to try out another method closer to the due date with family/friends.

## Playtesting, playtesting, playtesting and...playtesting! (60 min)
You will now spend the rest of class playtesting each other's levels.

Your instructor will split the class into two even groups: Group A and Group B. First, a member of Group A will playtest a member of Group B's level. Those same members will swap roles (Group B member will playtest Group A member's level). You'll then repeat this with a different pairing until everyone has done a fair bit of playtesting, and got a fair bit of data. It's best to collect as much data as you can!
